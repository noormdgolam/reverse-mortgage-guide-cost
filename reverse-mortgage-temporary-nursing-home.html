What Happens to Your Reverse Mortgage if You Move to a Nursing Home?

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: January 08, 2024
𝕏 Share f Share in Share P Pin

Key Takeaways

  • You can temporarily leave your home for medical reasons for up to 12 consecutive months.
  • If you are absent for 365 days, the loan becomes permanently Due and Payable.
  • You must still pay property taxes and insurance while you are in the facility.

A reverse mortgage is not a one-size-fits-all solution. Depending on your home's value, your age, and local property laws, the benefits can vary drastically. Let's explore exactly what you need to know.

A fundamental requirement of a Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM) is that the property must serve as your primary residence. You must live in the home for the majority of the calendar year.

But what happens if you suffer a severe fall or a stroke, and you have to be moved into a rehabilitation center or a skilled nursing facility? Does the bank instantly foreclose on your home?

The 12-Month Medical Exemption

The FHA recognizes that seniors face sudden medical emergencies. To protect borrowers, HUD guidelines include a specific "temporary absence" clause.

If you are the sole borrower on the loan, you can leave your home for physical or mental illness for up to 12 consecutive months without violating the primary residence requirement.

During this 12-month period, your reverse mortgage remains perfectly active. You will not be penalized, and the loan will not be called due.

The Two Critical Requirements During Absence

While you are recovering in a facility, you (or your Power of Attorney) must continue to fulfill the basic requirements of the loan:

  1. Property Taxes and Insurance: You must continue to pay your county property taxes and keep the home insured. If these lapse, the lender will begin foreclosure proceedings immediately, regardless of your medical status.
  2. Property Maintenance: The home cannot be abandoned to rot. Family members or a hired service must maintain the property to FHA standards (mowing the lawn, fixing leaks, preventing vandalism).

Day 366: The Loan is Called Due

The protection is strictly limited to 12 consecutive months.

If you have not moved back into the home by day 365, the FHA officially considers the property to be abandoned as a primary residence. On day 366, the reverse mortgage servicer will declare the loan Due and Payable.

At this point, your family (or Power of Attorney) must make arrangements to sell the home, or pay off the reverse mortgage balance to keep it.

(Note: If you have a co-borrower or an Eligible Non-Borrowing Spouse who is still living in the home while you are in the facility, the 12-month clock does not apply. The loan remains active indefinitely as long as one of you is still living there).

Crucial Eligibility Factors

  • Age Requirement: You typically must be at least 62 years old to qualify.
  • Home Equity: You must own your home outright or have a significant amount of equity built up.
  • Property Taxes & Insurance: Borrowers are still entirely responsible for ongoing property taxes, homeowners insurance, and general maintenance.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens when I pass away?

When the last surviving borrower permanently leaves the home, the loan becomes due. Your heirs can choose to sell the home to repay the loan, or refinance the home if they wish to keep it.
