Key Takeaways

  • If you are a victim of fraud, report the lender to the CFPB and the FTC immediately.
  • Contact your State Attorney General to initiate local legal action.
  • Do not ignore the problem; federal agencies aggressively prosecute reverse mortgage fraud.

\n\nThe reverse mortgage industry is heavily regulated, but bad actors still slip through the cracks. If a loan officer lied to you about the terms of the loan, forged your signature, or colluded with a shady contractor, you must take immediate action.

Do not suffer in silence. Federal regulators are extremely aggressive when it comes to prosecuting elder financial abuse.

Step 1: The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)

Your first and most powerful line of defense is the CFPB. This federal agency was created specifically to police the mortgage and banking industry.

You can file a formal complaint directly on their website (consumerfinance.gov) or by calling their toll-free number. When you file a complaint, the CFPB forwards it directly to the lender and demands a formal response within 15 days. Often, the mere threat of a CFPB investigation is enough to force a shady lender to correct their "mistake" and refund your money.

Step 2: The Federal Trade Commission (FTC)

If the fraud involves deceptive advertising (e.g., a mailer promising a "free house" or a "government grant"), you should report the company to the FTC. While the FTC does not resolve individual complaints, they look for patterns of fraud. If multiple seniors report the same company, the FTC will launch a massive federal lawsuit to shut the company down and secure refunds for the victims.

Step 3: Your State Attorney General

The mortgage industry is regulated at the state level. Your State Attorney General has the power to revoke a lender's license to operate within your state borders.

Visit your state's official website and look for the "Consumer Protection Division." Filing a complaint here can trigger a local, state-level investigation that moves much faster than a federal probe.

Step 4: Contact Your HUD Counselor

If you realize you have been scammed shortly after closing the loan, contact the independent HUD counselor you spoke with before the loan was approved. They can often provide you with emergency legal resources, including connecting you with pro-bono elder law attorneys in your area who specialize in unwinding fraudulent mortgages.\n
