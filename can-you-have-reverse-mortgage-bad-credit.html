Can You Get a Reverse Mortgage with Bad Credit?

Key Takeaways

  • There is no minimum FICO credit score required for a reverse mortgage.
  • Lenders care about your recent payment history, especially housing and tax debts.
  • Bad credit may require a mandatory Life Expectancy Set-Aside (LESA).

Can You Get a Reverse Mortgage with Bad Credit?

A reverse mortgage is a financial product that allows homeowners 62 years or older to borrow money using the equity they've built up in their home. It's a popular option for retirees looking to supplement their income or cover living expenses, but many individuals are concerned that a bad credit score will prevent them from qualifying.

The good news is that lenders do not check credit scores when approving reverse mortgage applications. This means that applicants with poor or bad credit can still qualify for a reverse mortgage, provided they meet the other eligibility requirements.

Credit history is not a factor in the reverse mortgage application process because these loans are not repaid through monthly payments like a traditional mortgage. Instead, homeowners are not required to repay the loan until they sell their home, move out, or pass away.

This lack of credit consideration is a key advantage of reverse mortgages for individuals with bad credit, as they are able to access a source of funds that might otherwise be unavailable to them.

That being said, applicants with poor credit may still face additional challenges when applying for a reverse mortgage. Some lenders may view these individuals as higher-risk borrowers and may offer less favorable terms, such as higher interest rates or lower loan-to-value ratios.

Additionally, homeowners who have outstanding debts or liens on their home may need to address these issues before applying for a reverse mortgage. This can include consolidating debts, paying off outstanding liens, or negotiating with creditors.

Reverse mortgage eligibility requires that homeowners be:

  • Absentee homeowners cannot get a regular reverse mortgage as they are required to live in the home.
  • At least 62 years old
  • Own their home free and clear, or have a low balance on their mortgage
  • Occupying their home as their primary residence
  • A U.S. citizen or permanent resident

Homeowners who meet these requirements may be able to access up to 80% of their home's value through a reverse mortgage, with the available funds being determined by the type of reverse mortgage they choose.

HECM (Home Equity Conversion Mortgage) is the most popular type of reverse mortgage, and it allows homeowners to borrow up to 60% of their home's value. Other types of reverse mortgages, such as private mortgage insurance (PMI) loans or proprietary reverse mortgages, may offer different loan-to-value ratios.

In summary, reverse mortgages do not require a credit check, which means individuals with bad credit can still qualify for this type of loan. However, poor credit may impact loan terms, making it essential for applicants to shop around and compare offers from multiple lenders.

It's crucial for prospective borrowers to educate themselves about the benefits and risks of reverse mortgages and to carefully evaluate their options before making a decision.
