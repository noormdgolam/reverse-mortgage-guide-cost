Reverse Mortgage Guide

A reverse mortgage is a type of home loan that allows homeowners aged 62 or older to borrow money using their home as collateral, without having to make monthly mortgage payments. It's a complex financial product that can be a good option for some, but not others. Understanding the ins and outs of reverse mortgages can help homeowners make an informed decision.

How Reverse Mortgages Work

Here's a step-by-step explanation of the reverse mortgage process:

  • You must be at least 62 years old and own your home or have a low balance on your mortgage.
  • You must live in your home as your primary residence.
  • You'll need to meet with a reverse mortgage counselor to discuss the loan and its risks and benefits.
  • You'll need to choose a reverse mortgage lender and review and sign a loan agreement.
  • The lender will conduct an appraisal of your home to determine its value.
  • The lender will calculate the amount of money you're eligible to borrow based on the appraised value of your home, your age, and current interest rates.

One of the main attractions of reverse mortgages is that they don't require you to make monthly mortgage payments. Instead, you'll receive a lump sum payment, monthly payments, or a line of credit that you can draw on as needed. The interest on the loan will accrue on the outstanding balance over time, reducing the equity in your home.

Types of Reverse Mortgages

There are three main types of reverse mortgages:

  • Single-Purpose Reverse Mortgage: These loans are offered by some non-profit and government agencies to pay for a specific expense, such as home repairs or property taxes.
  • Proprietary Reverse Mortgage: These loans are offered by private companies and often require a higher credit score and more equity in your home than HECM loans do.
  • HECM (Home Equity Conversion Mortgage) Reverse Mortgage: These loans are insured by the Federal Housing Administration and allow you to borrow up to 50% to 80% of your home's value, depending on your age and the value of your home.

Risks and Considerations

While reverse mortgages can be a good option for some homeowners, there are several risks and considerations to be aware of:

  • Accruing Interest: The interest on a reverse mortgage will accrue over time, reducing the equity in your home.
  • Taxes and Insurance: You'll still be responsible for paying property taxes and insurance on your home.
  • Home Maintenance: You'll be responsible for maintaining your home, making any necessary repairs, and paying for upkeep.
  • Impact on Medicaid and Social Security Benefits: A reverse mortgage may be considered a loan of a home equity line, which could impact your Medicaid or Social Security benefits.

If you're considering a reverse mortgage, it's essential to carefully weigh the pros and cons and consult with a financial advisor or housing counselor to determine if a reverse mortgage is right for you.
