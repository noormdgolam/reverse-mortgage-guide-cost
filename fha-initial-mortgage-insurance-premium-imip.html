Breaking Down the FHA Initial Mortgage Insurance Premium (IMIP)

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: February 08, 2024

Key Takeaways

  • The FHA IMIP is mandatory for all HECM reverse mortgages.
  • It costs exactly 2.00% of your home's appraised value (or the FHA lending limit).
  • This fee guarantees that the loan is non-recourse, protecting your heirs from debt.

Breaking Down the FHA Initial Mortgage Insurance Premium (IMIP) | Reverse Mortgage Guide

A reverse mortgage is a type of loan that allows homeowners, typically older adults, to borrow money using the equity in their home as collateral. In the United States, one of the most popular types of reverse mortgages is the FHA (Federal Housing Administration) reverse mortgage program. As part of this program, borrowers are required to pay an initial mortgage insurance premium (MIP), also known as the Initial Mortgage Insurance Premium (IMIP).

The IMIP is a one-time payment that is typically required at the time of loan closing. This premium is used to insure the lender against potential losses in the event that the borrower defaults on the loan. In the case of an FHA reverse mortgage, the borrower has the option of paying the IMIP upfront or folding it into the loan amount. The upfront option is usually chosen when the borrower has the funds available, while the folded option can be beneficial for those with limited cash reserves.

The cost of the FHA IMIP can vary depending on several factors, including the borrower's age, the loan amount, and the loan's interest rate. For FHA reverse mortgages, the IMIP is typically between 1.25% and 2% of the loan amount. For example, on a $200,000 loan, the IMIP would be $2,500 to $4,000.

  • Age-based premiums: The IMIP varies based on the borrower's age, with higher premiums for younger borrowers and lower premiums for older borrowers.
  • Loan amount-based premiums: The IMIP increases as the loan amount increases, with a maximum premium of 2% for loans over $500,000.
  • Interest rate-based premiums: The IMIP can also vary depending on the loan's interest rate, with higher premiums for loans with higher interest rates.

It's worth noting that there are some situations in which the borrower may be eligible for a refund of the IMIP. This can occur if the loan is paid off before the borrower passes away or if the borrower uses a HECM (Home Equity Conversion Mortgage) for Home Purchase loan. In these cases, some or all of the IMIP may be refundable, depending on the specific circumstances.

Understanding IMIP Refunding and Refund Options

Depending on the loan type and how the borrower chooses to use the funds, there may be some refund options available for the IMIP. Here are a few scenarios:

  • HECM for Purchase refunds: In some situations, a HECM for Purchase borrower may be eligible for a refund of the IMIP if the borrower pays off the loan within a certain time period (typically three years) after the loan closing.
  • Loan assumption refunds: If the borrower assumes the loan, the new borrower may be eligible for a refund of the IMIP if they pay it off within a certain time period (typically three years) after assuming the loan.
  • Default refund options: If the borrower defaults on the loan, the lender will generally refund a portion of the IMIP to the lender to cover any losses.

It's essential to consult with a qualified lender or financial advisor to understand your specific IMIP refund options based on your loan type and usage.

Conclusion

The FHA Initial Mortgage Insurance Premium (IMIP) is an essential consideration when evaluating a reverse mortgage. It provides critical insurance protection for lenders against potential losses but can impact the overall cost of the loan. By understanding the costs and refund options associated with the IMIP, borrowers can make more informed decisions about their reverse mortgage financing and ensure they are selecting the best option for their individual circumstances.

While the IMIP can seem like a significant upfront cost, it can also be a valuable tool for protecting borrowers from potential loss of their home. By working with a qualified lender or financial advisor, borrowers can navigate the complexities of the IMIP and make the most informed decisions about their reverse mortgage financing.
