Can Closing Costs Be Rolled Into the Reverse Mortgage Balance?

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: February 16, 2024
𝕏 Share f Share in Share P Pin

Key Takeaways

  • Almost all reverse mortgage closing costs can be financed (rolled into the loan).
  • This prevents house-rich, cash-poor seniors from having to drain their savings to get the loan.
  • Counseling fees and the initial appraisal fee are the only costs you usually pay out-of-pocket.

Can Closing Costs Be Rolled Into the Reverse Mortgage Balance?

For individuals interested in acquiring a reverse mortgage, one of the most pressing questions revolves around closing costs. This article will delve into the possibilities of incorporating these costs into the loan balance, while also providing an in-depth exploration of the associated considerations and guidelines.

Situations Where Closing Costs Can be Rolled In

  • Proprietary Reverse Mortgages: Proprietary reverse mortgage products often offer flexibility in loan structures, and in some cases, may include options for rolling closing costs into the loan balance.
  • Government-Backed Reverse Mortgages: In certain instances, especially when it comes to government-backed reverse mortgages like FHA-backed reverse mortgages (HECM), closing costs may not necessarily be directly rolled into the loan balance but rather absorbed by the government via insurance premiums.

Situations Where Closing Costs Cannot be Rolled In

  • HELOC and Other Credit Facility Reverse Mortgages: For Home Equity Conversion Loans (HELOC), reverse mortgage lines of credit, or proprietary lines of credit, the reverse mortgage lender or servicer may require borrowers to cover closing costs upfront.
  • Mortgage Insurance and Taxes on Proceeds: The costs of mortgage insurance premiums and taxes on the cash proceeds of a reverse mortgage may also not be factored into the loan balance, and thus must be paid separately by the borrower.

Considerations and Alternatives for Borrowers

When considering the possibility of incorporating closing costs into the reverse mortgage balance, several factors come into play.

  • Loan Amount and Collateral Value: The amount and value of the borrower's property are significant factors in determining the loan amount and whether closing costs can be added.
  • Financing Options: It's essential for borrowers to weigh the options of upfront closing costs versus the impact of rolled-in costs on their loan terms, including interest rates and loan duration.
  • Borrower Options for Costs: Borrowers might also explore alternative methods of funding the costs, such as home equity loans, personal savings, or gifts from family and friends.

It's essential for prospective borrowers to carefully discuss and consider their individual circumstances and options with their lender or financial advisor. They should explore the various mortgage products and understand that different programs come with varying rules regarding fees and costs.

Regulatory Considerations and Limitations

The ability of closing costs to be rolled into the loan depends on the specific regulations governing the loan type and the jurisdiction where the loan is being originated.

  • Government Regulation: Certain government-backed programs may limit the amount of costs that can be rolled into the loan, thereby requiring borrowers to pay costs separately.
  • Market Conditions: In a broader market context, changes in market conditions, such as changes in interest rates or lending policies, can influence a lender's willingness to roll closing costs into the loan balance.

This comprehensive look at the relationship between closing costs and reverse mortgage loan balance provides prospective borrowers with valuable insights into the complexities surrounding these costs.
