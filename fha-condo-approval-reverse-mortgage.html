FHA Condo Approval Secrets for Reverse Mortgages

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: January 22, 2024
𝕏 Share f Share in Share P Pin

Key Takeaways

  • The entire condo complex must usually be FHA-approved.
  • You can apply for a Single-Unit Approval (Spot Approval) if the complex is not approved.
  • The HOA's financial health will be heavily scrutinized by the lender.

Condominiums are incredibly popular among retirees seeking a maintenance-free lifestyle. However, getting a reverse mortgage on a condo is significantly more difficult than getting one on a single-family house.

This is because the FHA does not just evaluate your individual unit; they evaluate the financial and legal health of the entire Condominium Homeowners Association (HOA).

Full Project Approval

Historically, to get a Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM) on a condo, the entire condo complex had to be on the HUD Approved Condominium list.

If your HOA had let their FHA certification expire (which is common, as it costs money and requires an audit every few years), you could not get a reverse mortgage, period.

The Return of "Spot Approvals"

In 2019, HUD introduced a massive rule change that saved condo owners: Single-Unit Approvals (often called Spot Approvals).

If your condo complex is not FHA-approved, your lender can submit a massive packet of documents to HUD to get just your specific unit approved for a reverse mortgage.

The HOA Document Nightmare

While Spot Approvals are a lifesaver, they require the intense cooperation of your HOA board or property management company. Your reverse mortgage lender will require the HOA to fill out an exhaustive questionnaire and provide: 1. The HOA's current budget and balance sheet. 2. Proof that at least 10% of the HOA budget goes into a reserve account for future repairs. 3. Proof that no more than 15% of the units are 60+ days delinquent on their HOA dues. 4. Proof of a master insurance policy with sufficient hazard and liability coverage. 5. Details on any pending litigation against the HOA.

If your HOA is poorly managed, underfunded, or currently being sued by a contractor, the FHA will deny your Spot Approval, and your reverse mortgage will be declined.

Before paying hundreds of dollars for a reverse mortgage appraisal, always have your loan officer run a preliminary check on your HOA's financial health.
