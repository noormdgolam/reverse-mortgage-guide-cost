Leaving a Reverse Mortgaged Home to Charity

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: January 10, 2024
𝕏 Share f Share in Share P Pin

Key Takeaways

  • Yes, you can legally leave a reverse mortgaged home to a charity in your will.
  • The charity must pay off the loan balance to keep the home, or sell the home to extract the remaining equity.
  • Charities rarely want to keep real estate; they usually prefer liquid assets.

Many seniors without immediate heirs wish to leave their estate to a beloved church, university, or non-profit organization. If your primary asset is a home heavily encumbered by a reverse mortgage, can you still leave it to a charity?

The short answer is yes. You have the absolute legal right to bequeath the property to any entity you choose in your Last Will and Testament or Living Trust.

However, the logistics of how the charity receives the donation are complicated by the reverse mortgage.

The Charity Inherits the Debt

When you pass away, the charity inherits the home exactly the same way a human heir would. The charity does not receive a "free and clear" house; they receive a house with a massive lien attached to it.

The reverse mortgage becomes Due and Payable upon your death. The charity has a few options:

  1. Sell the Property (Most Common): Charities are rarely in the business of managing residential real estate. Typically, the charity's legal team will put the house on the market, sell it, use the proceeds to pay off the reverse mortgage balance, and deposit the remaining equity into the charity's general fund.
  2. Pay Off the Loan: If the charity specifically wants to use the house (e.g., as a parsonage or a retreat center), they must use their own organizational funds to write a check to the lender, paying off the reverse mortgage in full.

The Risk of Underwater Properties

If you live a very long time and the compounding interest of the reverse mortgage consumes all the equity in the home, the house may be "underwater" (you owe $300,000 but the home is only worth $250,000).

Because reverse mortgages are non-recourse loans, the charity is completely protected. They will not be forced to pay the $50,000 shortfall out of the charity's pockets. The charity will simply sign a Deed in Lieu of Foreclosure, handing the property back to the bank, and walk away.

However, in this scenario, the charity receives absolutely zero financial benefit from your estate.

If your primary goal is to leave a guaranteed, specific dollar amount to a charity, a reverse mortgage is the wrong tool. A reverse mortgage drains equity, making the final donation amount highly unpredictable.
