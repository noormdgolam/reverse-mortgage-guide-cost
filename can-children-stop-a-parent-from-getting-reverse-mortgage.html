Can Children Stop a Parent From Getting a Reverse Mortgage?

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: December 25, 2023
𝕏 Share f Share in Share P Pin

Key Takeaways

  • Adult children have no legal right to stop a competent parent from getting a reverse mortgage.
  • If the child is on the home's deed, they must consent to the loan.
  • A Power of Attorney cannot be used by a child to take out a reverse mortgage against the parent's will.

For many seniors, unlocking the equity in their homes is a powerful way to secure financial stability in retirement. In this comprehensive guide, we'll break down the specific rules and regulations that govern reverse mortgages in your area.

One of the most frequent sources of conflict surrounding a Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM) involves the adult children of the borrower.

Often, children discover that their parents are applying for a reverse mortgage and attempt to intervene, fearing they will lose their inheritance. This begs the legal question: Can adult children stop their parents from taking out a reverse mortgage?

The Legal Standard: Sole Ownership

If your parents are the sole owners of the home (meaning only their names are on the deed) and they are of sound mind, adult children have absolutely no legal right to stop them from getting a reverse mortgage.

The equity in the home belongs entirely to the parents. They are legally entitled to spend it, borrow against it, or give it away as they see fit. The FHA and the lender do not require the consent, notification, or approval of the borrower's children.

When Children Can Intervene

There are only three scenarios where adult children have legal standing to stop or alter a reverse mortgage application:

  1. The Child is on the Deed: If the parents added their adult child to the home's deed for estate planning purposes, the child is a legal co-owner. To get a reverse mortgage, all owners on the deed must either qualify as co-borrowers (which requires them to be at least 62) or agree to be removed from title. If the child refuses to sign a quitclaim deed, the loan cannot proceed.
  2. The Parent Lacks Mental Capacity: If a parent is suffering from severe dementia or Alzheimer's and cannot understand the loan they are signing, a child can intervene. The child would need to petition a court for conservatorship or invoke an existing durable Power of Attorney to halt the transaction based on lack of capacity.
  3. The Parent is in a Trust: If the home is in an irrevocable trust where the children are the trustees and hold decision-making power over the asset, the parents cannot take out a loan against the home without the trustees' consent (and FHA usually prohibits irrevocable trusts anyway).

If you are a child concerned about your parents' financial decisions, the best approach is an open, honest conversation rather than legal threats. Often, parents turn to reverse mortgages because they are secretly struggling with medical bills or living expenses and do not want to burden their children.

How Your Loan Limit is Calculated

Your maximum borrowing limit depends on the youngest borrower's age, the current interest rate, and the appraised value of your home (or the FHA lending limit, whichever is lower).

Frequently Asked Questions

Will a reverse mortgage affect my Social Security?

Generally, reverse mortgage proceeds are considered loan advances, not income. Therefore, they typically do not affect regular Social Security or Medicare benefits (though they may impact needs-based programs like Medicaid).
