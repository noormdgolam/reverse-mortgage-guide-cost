Deducting Reverse Mortgage Interest on Your Tax Return

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: December 08, 2023
𝕏 Share f Share in Share P Pin

Key Takeaways

  • Interest on a reverse mortgage is only deductible when it is actually paid.
  • Since reverse mortgages don't require monthly payments, you usually can't deduct interest annually.
  • The deduction is typically claimed as a lump sum when the loan is finally paid off.

Deducting Reverse Mortgage Interest on Your Tax Return | Reverse Mortgage Guide

A reverse mortgage is a loan that allows homeowners, typically 62 or older, to borrow money using the equity in their home as collateral. One of the benefits of a reverse mortgage is the tax implications, and homeowners may be eligible to deduct the interest paid on the loan from their tax return.

Understanding Reverse Mortgage Interest

When a homeowner takes out a reverse mortgage, they do not have to repay the loan during their lifetime, provided they continue to live in the home. The loan becomes due when the borrower passes away, sells the property, or moves out of the home. The interest on the loan accrues over time, and the borrower is required to pay it back when the loan becomes due.

The interest on a reverse mortgage is tax deductible for homeowners who itemize their tax deductions. This means that homeowners can report the interest paid on their tax return as a deduction, which can help reduce their taxable income.

Eligibility for Reverse Mortgage Interest Deduction

  • Homeowners must be at least 62 years old to qualify for a reverse mortgage.
  • The loan must be used as a primary residence or a second home.
  • Homeowners must report the interest paid on their tax return using Form 982, Basis of Property Recaptured.
  • Homeowners must itemize their tax deductions using Schedule A of IRS Form 1040.

Reporting Reverse Mortgage Interest on Your Tax Return

When reporting the interest paid on a reverse mortgage, homeowners must include it in one of two places on their tax return:

  1. As an adjustment to gross income (line 10b of Form 1040A or line 21 of Form 1040 for 2022 and previous tax years)
  2. As a mortgage interest deduction on Schedule A (Form 1040)

Homeowners must complete and attach Form 982 to their tax return to claim the deduction. Homeowners also must provide the lender's name, address, and the account number for the reverse mortgage.

Benefits of Deducting Reverse Mortgage Interest

Deducting the interest on a reverse mortgage can help reduce taxable income and minimize tax liability. This can be especially beneficial for homeowners who rely on their home equity to fund their retirement or face significant medical expenses.

Additionally, deducting the interest on a reverse mortgage can help homeowners preserve their retirement savings by reducing the amount of taxes owed on withdrawals from tax-deferred accounts.

Conclusion

Deducting the interest on a reverse mortgage can provide significant tax benefits for eligible homeowners. Homeowners should consult with a tax professional to determine the best way to report the interest paid on their tax return and maximize their tax savings.

It is essential for homeowners to understand the tax implications of a reverse mortgage and to maintain accurate records of interest paid to report it correctly on their tax return.
