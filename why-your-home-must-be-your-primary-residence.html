Why Your Home Must Be Your Primary Residence

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: March 25, 2024
𝕏 Share f Share in Share P Pin

Key Takeaways

  • FHA HECM loans are strictly forbidden on investment properties or vacation homes.
  • You must occupy the home for at least 183 days a year.
  • You must sign an Annual Occupancy Certificate swearing you live there.

Navigating the complex landscape of reverse mortgages can feel overwhelming. However, by understanding the localized laws and eligibility criteria, you can make an informed decision that protects your family's future.

The Federal Housing Administration (FHA) designed the reverse mortgage program to keep seniors in their homes as they age. It was not designed to help real estate investors leverage their rental portfolios.

Because of this, the most strictly enforced rule of the entire program is the Primary Residence Requirement.

The Definition of a Primary Residence

To qualify for—and maintain—a reverse mortgage, the property must be your principal residence. The FHA defines this as the home where you physically reside for the majority of the calendar year (at least 6 months and 1 day).

  • You Cannot Have Two Primary Residences: If you own a house in New York and a condo in Florida, you cannot have a reverse mortgage on both.
  • You Cannot Use It on a Vacation Home: If you spend 9 months a year at your primary house in Ohio, you cannot get a reverse mortgage on your summer cabin in Michigan.
  • You Cannot Use It on an Investment Property: You cannot buy a house, instantly rent it out to tenants, and take out a reverse mortgage on it.

The Annual Occupancy Certificate

How does the massive Wall Street corporation servicing your loan know that you actually live in a tiny house in Iowa?

Every single year, around the anniversary of your loan closing, the servicer will mail you an Annual Occupancy Certificate.

This is a legal document. You must sign it, date it, and mail it back. By signing it, you are swearing under penalty of federal perjury that the home is still your primary residence.

What Happens if You Move Out?

If you decide to move permanently to an assisted living facility, or if you buy an RV and spend 10 months a year traveling the country, the home is no longer your primary residence.

Once the home is no longer your primary residence, the reverse mortgage is officially over. The lender will declare the loan Due and Payable. You will have up to 6 months to sell the house, pay off the loan, and keep the remaining equity. If you refuse to sell, the lender will foreclose.

Crucial Eligibility Factors

  • Age Requirement: You typically must be at least 62 years old to qualify.
  • Home Equity: You must own your home outright or have a significant amount of equity built up.
  • Property Taxes & Insurance: Borrowers are still entirely responsible for ongoing property taxes, homeowners insurance, and general maintenance.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens when I pass away?

When the last surviving borrower permanently leaves the home, the loan becomes due. Your heirs can choose to sell the home to repay the loan, or refinance the home if they wish to keep it.
