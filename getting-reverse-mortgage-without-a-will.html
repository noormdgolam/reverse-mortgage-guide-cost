Getting a Reverse Mortgage if You Don't Have a Will

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: May 10, 2024
𝕏 Share f Share in Share P Pin

Key Takeaways

  • You are not legally required to have a Will to get a reverse mortgage.
  • Dying intestate (without a Will) throws your home into a lengthy probate process.
  • A long probate often results in the lender foreclosing before your heirs can sell the home.

You do not need a Last Will and Testament to get approved for a reverse mortgage. The bank does not care what happens to your assets after you die; they only care that their lien is secured.

However, taking out a reverse mortgage without having a concrete estate plan in place is one of the most selfish financial mistakes a senior can make.

The Probate Nightmare

When you pass away, the reverse mortgage instantly becomes Due and Payable. The lender will issue a 6-month deadline for your heirs to either pay off the loan balance or sell the home.

If you die intestate (without a Will or a Trust), your home is thrown into your state's probate court. A judge must legally determine who your rightful heirs are and appoint an executor to manage your estate.

The Ticking Clock vs. The Court

Here is the fatal flaw: Probate courts are incredibly slow. It can easily take 8 to 12 months for a judge to officially grant your children the legal authority (Letters Testamentary) to sell your house or refinance the mortgage.

The reverse mortgage lender's clock is ticking much faster. If 6 months pass, and your children still don't have the legal authority to list the house for sale because the probate court is dragging its feet, the lender will initiate foreclosure proceedings.

Your children will watch helplessly as the bank forecloses on the family home and wipes out any remaining equity, simply because they didn't have the legal paperwork required to stop it.

The Solution

If you are going to take out a massive debt that comes due upon your death, you must give your heirs the legal tools to handle it efficiently.

Before closing on a reverse mortgage, spend $500 with a local estate attorney to draft a simple Will, or place the home into a Revocable Living Trust. A trust completely bypasses probate court, allowing your children to instantly assume control of the property the day you pass away, giving them plenty of time to sell the house and extract your remaining equity before the bank forecloses.
