The 5 Most Common Reasons Reverse Mortgages Are Denied

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: May 16, 2024
𝕏 Share f Share in Share P Pin

Key Takeaways

  • Not having enough equity is the #1 reason for denial.
  • Failing the residual income test can kill the loan if a LESA isn't possible.
  • Severe property damage and title issues are common deal-breakers.

The 5 Most Common Reasons Reverse Mortgages Are Denied | Reverse Mortgage Guide

A reverse mortgage can be a valuable tool for homeowners 62 years or older who are struggling to make monthly mortgage payments or need access to cash for personal expenses. However, these loans come with certain requirements and qualifications, and borrowers may be denied if they do not meet them. Here are the 5 most common reasons why reverse mortgage applications are denied:

Lack of Home Equity

One of the primary requirements for a reverse mortgage is a sufficient amount of home equity. Lenders need to ensure that there is enough value in the property to cover the loan balance, interest, and fees. If the home's value is less than the outstanding mortgage balance, or if the mortgage is already heavily outstanding, the lender may deny the application. Additionally, if the borrower's credit score or other financial factors make them a higher risk, the lender may also deny the application.

Insufficient Age or Income Requirements

As mentioned earlier, borrowers must be 62 years or older to qualify for a reverse mortgage. Lenders often verify a borrower's age and Social Security number to confirm their eligibility. Additionally, some lenders may require borrowers to demonstrate a steady income or a certain level of creditworthiness. If a borrower does not meet these requirements, their application may be denied.

Inaccurate or Incomplete Applications

Reverse mortgage lenders are diligent in reviewing every detail of the application process to prevent potential fraud and ensure compliance with government regulations. A single incorrect or missing item on the application can lead to denial. The application process typically requires information about the borrower's income, expenses, credit history, and loan-to-value ratio, among other factors. Any inaccuracies or omissions could lead the lender to deny the application.

Unpaid Outstanding Fees

Reverse mortgage lenders may deny an application if the borrower has any outstanding fees associated with the property, such as outstanding property taxes, homeowner association fees, or unpaid loan balances. Lenders require a clean title to prevent potential foreclosure and protect their interests. If the borrower has any outstanding fees, it may be essential to resolve these issues before proceeding with the reverse mortgage application.

Inadequate Title and Property Ownership

Borrowers who are not listed as owners on the property, such as those who are listed as tenants, co-signers, or co-borrowers, may face denial due to inadequate title and property ownership. It is crucial for borrowers to demonstrate their ownership in the property, and lenders will verify this through title search and county records. Lenders may also deny the application if the property has outstanding liens, such as tax liens or other creditors, or if it is already subject to another mortgage or home equity loan.

While obtaining a reverse mortgage can provide much-needed cash for personal expenses, it is a long-term loan with significant implications. Borrowers should carefully review their financial situation and consider their options before applying for a reverse mortgage. For those who do decide to apply, understanding the most common reasons for denial can help them navigate the process more confidently and effectively.
