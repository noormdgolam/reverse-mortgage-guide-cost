Buying Out Siblings After Inheriting a Reverse Mortgage Home

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: December 27, 2023
𝕏 Share f Share in Share P Pin

Key Takeaways

  • The sibling keeping the home must pay off the entire reverse mortgage balance.
  • They must also pay the other siblings for their share of the remaining equity.
  • This usually requires taking out a new traditional mortgage in the purchasing sibling's name.

Understanding the Options after Inheriting a Reverse Mortgage Home

Receiving an inheritance from a loved one can be both a blessing and a challenge, especially if the inherited asset is a house with an existing reverse mortgage. A reverse mortgage is a loan program that allows homeowners 62 and older to borrow funds secured by the equity in their homes. However, when the homeowner passes away, the heirs may face a daunting task in managing the loan obligations.

What are the Consequences of Inheriting a Reverse Mortgage?

When inheriting a reverse mortgage, the surviving family members may face significant challenges, including:

  • Liquidating the property to pay off the loan balance
  • Paying off the loan in one lump sum
  • Assuming the mortgage payments
  • Sale-leaseback arrangements

Should I Buy Out My Siblings?

In cases where siblings have differing ideas about how to manage the inherited property, buying out their siblings may be a viable option. This can provide peace of mind and clarity on how to proceed.

Before making a decision, it's essential to understand the pros and cons of buying out siblings. Some of the advantages include:

  • Ensuring you are in control of the property's management
  • Potentially avoiding disputes with siblings
  • Allowing you to focus on maintaining the property

How to Buy Out Siblings after Inheriting a Reverse Mortgage

If buying out siblings is the best decision, here are some steps to consider:

  1. Determine the value of the property: Hire an appraiser to determine the current market value of the property.
  2. Calculate the loan balance: Obtain a loan statement from the lender to determine the amount owed on the reverse mortgage.
  3. Assess the cost of selling the property: Factor in any expenses associated with selling the property, such as real estate commissions and closing costs.
  4. Consult with an attorney: Review the will and any relevant estate planning documents to understand the distribution of assets and any potential liabilities.
  5. Purchase the property from siblings: Negotiate a price with your siblings to purchase the property, or use a court-ordered buyout if necessary.
  6. Court-ordered buyouts: If disagreements with siblings cannot be resolved, a court-ordered buyout may be necessary.

Conclusion

Buying out siblings after inheriting a reverse mortgage can be a complex process. Before making any decisions, it's crucial to understand the pros and cons and the steps involved in the process. Consult with financial advisors, attorneys, and appraisers to ensure you are making an informed decision that aligns with your goals and objectives.
