Key Takeaways

  • You cannot use a HECM for Purchase to directly buy a second home.
  • You can use cash withdrawn from your primary residence's reverse mortgage to buy another property.
  • However, doing so drastically drains your equity and creates massive financial risk.

\n\nMany seniors dream of spending their retirement winters in Florida or Arizona. But if all your wealth is tied up in your primary residence in Ohio, buying a second home seems impossible.

A common question arises: Can I use a reverse mortgage to buy a vacation home?

The answer requires careful distinction between what is legally allowed and what is financially wise.

The HECM for Purchase Rule

The FHA offers a specific program called HECM for Purchase, which allows you to buy a new house using a reverse mortgage in a single transaction.

However, the FHA strictly prohibits using the HECM for Purchase program to buy a second home, a vacation home, or an investment property. The home you are purchasing must become your primary residence within 60 days of closing.

The Loophole: Using Cash Proceeds

While you cannot use the specific HECM for Purchase program for a vacation home, there is a legal workaround.

If you already have a reverse mortgage on your primary residence in Ohio, you are legally allowed to withdraw a massive lump sum from your available line of credit. Once that cash is in your personal checking account, HUD does not dictate how you spend it.

You are legally permitted to take $200,000 from your Ohio reverse mortgage and use it to pay cash for a condo in Florida.

The Massive Financial Danger

Just because it is legal does not mean it is a good idea. Using a reverse mortgage to buy a second home is considered one of the riskiest financial maneuvers a senior can make.

  1. Double the Expenses: You now have to pay property taxes, homeowners insurance, and HOA dues on two properties. If you fail to pay the taxes on your Ohio home, the reverse mortgage will go into default, and you could lose both houses.
  2. Accelerated Equity Drain: Taking a $200,000 lump sum instantly triggers massive compounding interest. Your loan balance in Ohio will explode, quickly wiping out any remaining equity you intended to leave to your heirs.
  3. The 6-Month Occupancy Rule: Remember, the Ohio home with the reverse mortgage must remain your primary residence. You must live in Ohio for at least 183 days a year. If you fall in love with Florida and spend 7 months there, you are violating the primary residence rule, and the Ohio loan will be called due and payable.\n
