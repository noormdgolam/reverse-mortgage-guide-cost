To maintain a reverse mortgage in good standing, you are legally required to keep the home fully insured against fire and hazards. 

Every year, your loan servicer will contact your insurance agent to verify that the policy has been renewed. But what happens if you forgot to pay the bill, or your insurance company dropped you because you live in a high-fire-risk zone?

If the servicer discovers your home is uninsured, they will initiate the most expensive penalty in the mortgage industry: **Forced Placed Insurance**.

## The Cost of Forced Placed Insurance

To protect their collateral, the servicer will immediately go to a commercial insurance broker and purchase a policy on your house. 

Because the servicer is buying this policy blindly without inspecting the home, the premiums are astronomical. **Forced Placed Insurance typically costs two to three times more than a standard homeowners policy.**

You do not write a check for this massive premium. The servicer simply adds the $4,000 cost directly to your reverse mortgage loan balance, where it immediately begins accruing 7% compounding interest. 

## The Ultimate Insult: It Doesn't Protect You

The most devastating aspect of Forced Placed Insurance is the coverage itself. 

The policy is written explicitly to protect the *lender's financial interest*, not yours. 
- It covers the physical structure of the house.
- **It does NOT cover your personal belongings.** If the house burns down, your furniture, jewelry, and electronics are entirely uninsured.
- **It does NOT provide liability coverage.** If the mailman slips on your icy driveway and sues you, you have no legal protection.

## How to Fix It

If you receive a letter stating that Forced Placed Insurance has been added to your loan, you must act immediately. 

Call an independent insurance broker and purchase a standard, affordable homeowners policy. The moment you fax the new "Declarations Page" to your reverse mortgage servicer proving you have your own coverage, they will cancel the expensive Forced Placed policy and issue a prorated refund to your loan balance.
