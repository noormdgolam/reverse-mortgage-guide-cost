The Federal Housing Administration (FHA) insures your reverse mortgage, which means your home is their collateral. Because of this, the FHA requires the home to meet strict Minimum Property Standards (MPS) regarding health, safety, and structural integrity.

When the FHA appraiser inspects your home, they are not just looking at the value; they are looking for peeling lead paint, a failing roof, exposed wiring, or a broken HVAC system.

What happens if the appraiser flags these issues? Do you have to pay out of pocket to fix them before you can get the loan?

## The Repair Set-Aside Solution

If the repairs are relatively minor (typically under $5,000) and do not pose an immediate, catastrophic threat to the structure, the FHA allows the loan to close using a **Repair Set-Aside (Repair Rider)**.

You do not have to pay for the repairs out of pocket before closing. Instead, you agree to fix the issues *after* the loan closes, using the money from the reverse mortgage itself.

## How the Math Works

To guarantee the work gets done, the lender will freeze a portion of your reverse mortgage funds. The FHA requires lenders to hold back **150% of the estimated repair cost.**

**Example:**
- The appraiser notes peeling paint and a broken window.
- The contractor estimates it will cost $2,000 to fix.
- At closing, the lender sets aside $3,000 (150% of $2k) in a frozen account.
- You receive the rest of your reverse mortgage funds.
- You have up to 6 months to hire a contractor and complete the repairs.
- Once the repairs are done, the appraiser returns for a final inspection.
- The lender pays the contractor the $2,000, and the remaining $1,000 is released back into your available line of credit.

## Major Structural Issues

Repair Set-Asides are only for minor to moderate issues. If the appraiser determines the home has a sinking foundation, a roof that is actively caving in, or an active termite infestation destroying the load-bearing walls, the FHA will not allow a Repair Set-Aside. 

In these severe cases, the home is deemed uninsurable. You must find a way to finance and complete the repairs yourself, out of pocket, before the reverse mortgage can proceed to closing.
