One of the most devastating financial shocks a retired couple can face is the sudden need for long-term care. With in-home nursing care often exceeding $5,000 a month and memory care facilities costing upwards of $8,000 a month, savings accounts can be wiped out in a matter of months.

For couples who are house-rich but cash-poor, a reverse mortgage is frequently used as a financial lifeline to fund this care.

## Funding In-Home Care

The most ideal scenario is keeping the ailing spouse in the comfort of their own home. 
A couple can establish a Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM) line of credit and draw from it monthly to pay for visiting nurses, physical therapists, or daily caregivers. 

Because the proceeds are tax-free, they do not trigger income taxes or affect Social Security benefits, making it an highly efficient way to pay medical bills.

## When One Spouse Moves to a Facility

If the ailing spouse's condition deteriorates and they must be moved to a skilled nursing facility, the reverse mortgage provides a critical layer of protection for the healthy spouse.

Under FHA rules, as long as at least one co-borrower continues to live in the home as their primary residence, the reverse mortgage remains in good standing. The healthy spouse can remain in the family home, while drawing from the reverse mortgage line of credit to pay the monthly bills for their partner's nursing facility.

## The Major Risk: Draining the Equity

While a reverse mortgage is a powerful tool to solve an immediate medical crisis, couples must carefully consider the long-term impact on the surviving, healthy spouse.

If a couple burns through $150,000 of home equity in three years to pay for nursing care before the ailing spouse passes away, that equity is permanently gone. The surviving spouse is now left living in a home with a massive, compounding reverse mortgage balance and very little available credit remaining. 

If the surviving spouse later needs long-term care for themselves, or simply needs cash to fix a broken furnace, the safety net is gone. 

Before using a reverse mortgage for long-term care, families should consult an elder law attorney to determine if the ailing spouse could qualify for Medicaid to cover the facility costs, thereby preserving the home equity for the healthy spouse.
