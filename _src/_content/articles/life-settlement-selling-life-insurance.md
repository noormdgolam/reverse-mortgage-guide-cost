Many seniors in their 70s and 80s are holding onto massive whole life or universal life insurance policies that they purchased decades ago to protect their young children. 

Now, the children are wealthy adults, the mortgage is paid off, and the senior is struggling to pay the expensive monthly premiums on that exact same life insurance policy. To survive financially, the senior considers a reverse mortgage on their home.

Before touching your home equity, you must look at your life insurance. You can sell it in a transaction known as a **Life Settlement**.

## How a Life Settlement Works

Life insurance is a legally transferable asset. You have the right to sell the policy to a third-party institutional investor.

1. **The Sale:** You sell your $500,000 life insurance policy to a Wall Street firm. 
2. **The Payout:** The firm pays you a massive lump sum of cash today. The payout is usually vastly higher than the "cash surrender value" the insurance company offers you, but less than the full $500,000 death benefit. (e.g., they might pay you $150,000).
3. **The Transfer:** The investor takes over the policy. They pay the monthly premiums to keep it active. When you eventually pass away, the investor receives the $500,000 death benefit.

## Why This Beats a Reverse Mortgage

If you need a lump sum of $150,000 to fund your retirement, a life settlement is often a dramatically better choice than a reverse mortgage.

- **You Protect the House:** Your home equity remains untouched. You leave the house free and clear to your heirs.
- **You Eliminate an Expense:** By selling the policy, you no longer have to pay the expensive monthly life insurance premiums, instantly improving your monthly cash flow.
- **No Loan to Repay:** A reverse mortgage is a loan that accrues compounding interest. A life settlement is a flat sale. You owe nothing, and the transaction is complete.

## The Caveat

To qualify for a lucrative life settlement, you generally must be over the age of 70, and you must have some sort of health impairment. Investors buy these policies based on life expectancy calculations; if you are 70 and in Olympic-level perfect health, investors will not buy the policy because they would have to pay the premiums for 30 years before seeing a return.
