The federally insured Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM) is the standard for 90% of the reverse mortgage market. However, the HECM program has a fatal flaw for seniors living in extremely expensive real estate markets like California, New York, or Hawaii.

The FHA imposes a strict national **Maximum Claim Amount limit (currently $1,149,825 for 2024).**

If your home in San Francisco is appraised at $3 million, the FHA treats it as if it is only worth $1.14 million. You leave nearly two million dollars of equity on the table, completely inaccessible.

The solution is the **Proprietary Jumbo Reverse Mortgage**.

## What is a Jumbo Reverse Mortgage?

Jumbo reverse mortgages are private loans backed by massive banks and investment firms, not the federal government. Because they operate outside FHA rules, lenders can set their own lending limits—often allowing you to access equity on homes valued up to $4 million or even $10 million.

## The Advantages of Jumbo Loans

1. **Access to More Cash:** If you have a $2.5 million home, a Jumbo loan might allow you to draw $1 million in tax-free cash, compared to a maximum of roughly $400k on a HECM.
2. **No FHA Mortgage Insurance:** HECM loans require you to pay a massive upfront Mortgage Insurance Premium (2% of the home's value) to the FHA. Jumbo loans are private; they do not charge this premium, saving you tens of thousands of dollars in closing costs.
3. **Condo Flexibility:** Jumbo lenders do not require strict FHA condo project approval. If your luxury condo was rejected by the FHA, a Jumbo lender might approve it immediately.

## The Disadvantages

1. **Higher Interest Rates:** Because Jumbo lenders do not have the safety net of the federal government, they take on more risk. They compensate for this by charging higher interest rates than a standard HECM.
2. **Fewer Payout Options:** HECM loans are famous for their flexible Line of Credit that grows over time. Jumbo loans typically require you to take the money as a single massive lump sum at closing, which begins compounding interest immediately. 
3. **Stricter Age Requirements:** While you can get a HECM at age 62, many Jumbo lenders require the youngest borrower to be at least 60, and sometimes they offer worse loan-to-value ratios unless you are in your 70s.
