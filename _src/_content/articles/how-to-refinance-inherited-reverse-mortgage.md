If you inherit a home that has an active reverse mortgage, and you decide you want to keep the property, you must pay off the loan balance. Because most heirs do not have hundreds of thousands of dollars in cash, the standard procedure is to refinance the home into a new, traditional mortgage.

Because the original borrower has passed away, the process is slightly different than a standard refinance.

## You Cannot Assume the Loan

The first thing heirs must understand is that **a reverse mortgage is not assumable.** 

You cannot simply call the lender, give them your name, and take over the loan where your parents left off. The loan is permanently tied to the age and life expectancy of the original borrowers. When they pass away, the loan is called Due and Payable.

## The Refinance Process

To keep the home, you must pay off the reverse mortgage using funds from a new loan that you take out in your own name.

1. **Notify the Servicer:** Call the reverse mortgage company immediately and inform them of the death. Tell them your explicit intent is to keep the property and refinance. They will grant you an initial 6-month period to complete this process.
2. **Transfer the Title:** You cannot take out a mortgage on a home you don't own. You must work with a probate attorney to ensure the deed is legally transferred into your name (or the name of the trust you control).
3. **Apply for a Traditional Mortgage:** Go to a local bank or mortgage broker. You will apply for a traditional conventional or FHA loan. 
4. **Qualify:** You must qualify for this new mortgage based entirely on *your* credit score, *your* income, and *your* debt-to-income ratio. The bank does not care how much equity your parents had; they care if you can afford the monthly payments on the new loan.
5. **Close the Loan:** At closing, your new lender wires the funds directly to the reverse mortgage servicer, paying the debt in full. You now own the home and will begin making monthly mortgage payments to your new lender.

## The 95% Rule

What if the reverse mortgage balance is $300,000, but the home is only worth $200,000? 

The FHA has a special protection for heirs. If the home is "underwater," heirs who want to keep the property only have to pay **95% of the current appraised value.** 

In this scenario, you would only need to secure a new mortgage for $190,000 (95% of $200k). The FHA insurance fund pays the lender the remaining $110,000 shortfall.
