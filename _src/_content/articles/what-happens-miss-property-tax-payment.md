---\n\nA reverse mortgage eliminates your monthly mortgage payment, but it does **not** eliminate your obligation to pay property taxes, homeowners insurance, and HOA dues. 

Failing to pay property taxes is considered a breach of the loan agreement and is the number one reason seniors lose their homes to reverse mortgage foreclosure.

## The Timeline of a Missed Payment

If you miss your county property tax deadline, the county will eventually place a tax lien on your property. This is a massive problem for the reverse mortgage lender because, legally, a county tax lien takes priority over the lender's mortgage lien. 

Here is what happens when you miss a payment:

### 1. The Warning Letter
Your loan servicer actively monitors county tax records. If they see your taxes are delinquent, they will send you a strongly worded letter demanding that you pay the taxes immediately and provide proof of payment.

### 2. Corporate Advances
If you do not pay, the lender will not allow the county to seize the house. Instead, the lender will pay the property taxes on your behalf to protect their lien position. This is called a "corporate advance."

The lender will then add the cost of those taxes to your reverse mortgage loan balance.

### 3. Calling the Loan Due
A corporate advance is not a favor; it is a desperate measure by the lender. Once they pay your taxes, they will declare your loan in **Technical Default**. 

You will be given an opportunity to repay the lender for the taxes they fronted. If you cannot repay them, the lender will call the loan due and payable, initiating formal foreclosure proceedings to sell the house and recoup their money.

## How to Prevent This: The LESA

To prevent tax defaults, HUD created the **Life Expectancy Set-Aside (LESA)**.

During the application process, the lender performs a Financial Assessment. If they determine you do not have enough residual income to safely afford your property taxes over your expected lifespan, they will require a LESA.

A LESA takes a large chunk of your available reverse mortgage funds (often tens of thousands of dollars) and locks it in an escrow account. The lender then uses that locked money to automatically pay your property taxes for you every year. 

While a LESA drastically reduces the cash you receive upfront, it provides absolute peace of mind that your taxes will be paid and you will never face foreclosure for a missed payment.\n
