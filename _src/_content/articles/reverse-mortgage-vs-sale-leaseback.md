As property values have skyrocketed, a new industry of Wall Street investors and private equity firms has emerged, targeting cash-poor seniors. They advertise a product called a **Sale-Leaseback**.

The marketing pitch sounds similar to a reverse mortgage: *Get the cash out of your equity without having to move out of your house.*

However, the legal mechanics—and the risks—are completely different.

## How a Reverse Mortgage Works

With a Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM), you take out a loan against your equity.
- **Ownership:** You remain the legal owner on the deed.
- **Appreciation:** If the home value goes up, you (or your heirs) keep that profit.
- **Security:** As long as you pay property taxes and insurance, you cannot be evicted. The FHA guarantees your right to live there for life.

## How a Sale-Leaseback Works

With a sale-leaseback, you are not taking out a loan. You are **selling your home** to a private investment firm.
- **Ownership:** You surrender the deed. The investor is the new owner. You are now a tenant.
- **The Cash:** The investor pays you cash for the home (often below market value).
- **The Catch:** You sign a lease agreeing to pay the investor monthly rent to continue living in the house you used to own.

## The Massive Risks of Sale-Leasebacks

While sale-leasebacks can close faster than a heavily regulated FHA reverse mortgage, they are incredibly dangerous for seniors on fixed incomes.

1. **Eviction Risk:** You are now a renter. If your rent goes up in three years and you can no longer afford it, the investor will evict you from the home you lived in for 30 years. 
2. **Loss of Appreciation:** Because you no longer own the home, any future appreciation belongs to the Wall Street firm, not your heirs.
3. **No FHA Protections:** Reverse mortgages are heavily regulated by the federal government to protect seniors. Sale-leasebacks are largely unregulated real estate transactions.

A sale-leaseback should only be considered as an absolute last resort if you cannot qualify for a reverse mortgage due to credit failures, or if the home does not meet FHA property standards. If you have the choice, retaining ownership via a reverse mortgage is vastly safer.
