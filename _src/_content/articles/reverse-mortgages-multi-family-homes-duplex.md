Most people associate reverse mortgages exclusively with single-family suburban houses. However, the Federal Housing Administration (FHA) allows seniors to use a Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM) on multi-family properties.

If you own a duplex, triplex, or a four-plex, you have a unique opportunity to access your home equity while continuing to generate rental income.

## The Primary Residence Requirement

The golden rule for getting a reverse mortgage on a multi-family property is occupancy. 

You cannot use a HECM on an investment property where you are an absentee landlord. **You must live in one of the units as your primary residence.** If you own a triplex, you must occupy Unit A, and you are free to rent out Unit B and Unit C. 

If you ever move out of your primary unit (e.g., to a nursing home or to live with your children), the entire reverse mortgage will become Due and Payable, and you will be forced to sell the building.

## Using Rental Income to Qualify

Following the 2015 Financial Assessment rules, all reverse mortgage applicants must prove they have the income to continue paying their property taxes and homeowners insurance.

If you own a multi-family building, the FHA allows lenders to count the **verifiable rental income** generated by the other units toward your financial qualification. 

If your Social Security alone is not enough to cover the massive property tax bill on a 4-unit building in Chicago, the monthly rent checks you receive from your three tenants will usually push your income high enough to pass the Financial Assessment, avoiding a mandatory Life Expectancy Set-Aside (LESA).

## Maintenance Requirements

Being a landlord comes with maintenance responsibilities. A reverse mortgage adds an extra layer of strictness. 

The FHA requires the entire property—not just your unit—to be maintained to minimum safety standards. If a tenant trashes one of your rental units, or if the roof over the rental units starts leaking, you must repair it. Failing to maintain the property can trigger a default on your reverse mortgage.
