If you live in a condominium or a planned suburban community, you pay a monthly fee to a Homeowners Association (HOA). While this fee covers landscaping and neighborhood pools, it is a massive liability when applying for a reverse mortgage.

The FHA views HOA dues as a critical threat to the financial stability of the loan.

## The Threat to Residual Income

During the underwriting process, you must pass the Financial Assessment by proving you have enough "Residual Income" left over at the end of the month to buy groceries and medicine.

**HOA dues are deducted directly from your available income during this calculation.**

If you have a modest Social Security income of $1,800 a month, and your luxury condo charges a massive $600/month HOA fee, that fee obliterates your cash flow in the eyes of the underwriter. It is highly likely you will fail the Residual Income test, resulting in a mandatory Life Expectancy Set-Aside (LESA) or an outright denial.

## The Foreclosure Threat (Super Liens)

Why does the FHA care so much about your HOA? 

In nearly two dozen states, Homeowners Associations are granted **"Super Lien" status**. This means that if you fail to pay your HOA dues, the HOA has the legal right to foreclose on your home to collect the debt.

More terrifyingly for the bank, a Super Lien takes priority over the first mortgage. If the HOA forecloses and sells the condo at auction for pennies on the dollar, the HOA gets their money, and the massive reverse mortgage lien is completely wiped out. The bank loses everything.

Because of this terrifying legal reality, reverse mortgage servicers monitor HOA payments aggressively. If you stop paying your HOA dues, the reverse mortgage servicer will step in, pay the HOA on your behalf, and immediately begin foreclosing on you to protect their collateral. 

When budgeting for a reverse mortgage, you must ensure you have the cash flow to handle potential HOA fee increases over the next 20 years.
