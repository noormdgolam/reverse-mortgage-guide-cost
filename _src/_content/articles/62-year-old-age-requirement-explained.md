The most famous rule of the reverse mortgage industry is the age requirement: **You must be at least 62 years old.** 

This rule is not set by individual banks; it is a federal law written into the National Housing Act, which created the Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM) program. But why 62, and how strictly is it enforced?

## The Actuarial Math Behind Age 62

A reverse mortgage is a loan based entirely on life expectancy. Because you are not making monthly payments, the lender must calculate exactly how long you are likely to live in the home before the loan is paid off. 

At age 62 (the same age you become eligible for early Social Security), the actuarial tables suggest you have roughly 20 to 25 years of life expectancy remaining. This timeline allows the FHA to accurately calculate how much interest will compound over the life of the loan.

If a 40-year-old took out a reverse mortgage, they might live in the home for another 50 years. The compounding interest over five decades would be mathematically guaranteed to exceed the value of the home by millions of dollars, instantly bankrupting the FHA insurance fund.

## What if You Turn 62 Next Month?

Lenders are incredibly strict. You cannot close the loan when you are 61 and 11 months old. 

However, you can *start* the application process early. Because a reverse mortgage takes roughly 45 to 60 days to process, lenders will often allow you to begin the paperwork, take the HUD counseling, and order the appraisal when you are 61 and a half. But the final closing documents cannot be signed until your actual 62nd birthday.

## The Younger Spouse Exception

What if you are 65, but your wife is 58? 

Historically, you would have to remove your wife from the deed to get the loan, leaving her completely unprotected if you died. Today, the FHA allows the 58-year-old to remain in the home as an **Eligible Non-Borrowing Spouse (NBS)**. 

Because she is under 62, her age will heavily restrict how much money you can borrow (the younger the spouse, the less cash you get), but she is legally guaranteed the right to live in the home for the rest of her life, even after you pass away.
