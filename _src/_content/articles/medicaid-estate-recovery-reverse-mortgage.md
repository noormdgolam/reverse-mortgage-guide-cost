Many seniors rely on Medicaid to pay for long-term care or nursing home expenses. What many families do not realize is that Medicaid operates like a massive loan. 

Under federal law, every state is required to operate a **Medicaid Estate Recovery Program (MERP)**. When a Medicaid beneficiary passes away, the state must attempt to recover the costs of the care it provided by placing a claim against the deceased person's estate—most notably, their home.

How does this interact with a Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM)?

## Lien Priority: Who Gets Paid First?

When a senior with both a reverse mortgage and a history of Medicaid benefits passes away, two massive entities want to be repaid from the sale of the house: the bank (HUD/FHA) and the state government (Medicaid).

In property law, the rule is generally "first in time, first in right." 

Because the reverse mortgage was recorded as a lien on the property *before* the senior passed away and triggered the MERP claim, **the reverse mortgage lender is always in the first lien position.**

When the home is sold, the reverse mortgage balance must be paid off in full before Medicaid sees a single dime.

## What Happens to the Remaining Equity?

If the home sells for $300,000, and the reverse mortgage payoff is $200,000, there is $100,000 of equity remaining.

In a normal scenario, that $100,000 would go to the heirs. However, if the state has filed a MERP claim for $150,000 in nursing home costs, **the state will seize the remaining $100,000 of equity.** The heirs will receive nothing.

Because the state's claim ($150,000) was larger than the remaining equity ($100,000), the state absorbs the $50,000 loss. They cannot sue the heirs for the difference.

## Strategic Considerations for Families

Some families attempt to use a reverse mortgage specifically to drain the equity out of the home *before* Medicaid can seize it. If the senior draws all the cash from the reverse mortgage, there will be no equity left in the home when they die, leaving Medicaid with nothing to recover.

However, if the senior takes that cash and gives it to their children, it will violate Medicaid's strict **5-year look-back rule**, resulting in a massive penalty period where the senior will be denied Medicaid coverage. 

Mixing reverse mortgages and Medicaid requires the guidance of a licensed elder law attorney.
