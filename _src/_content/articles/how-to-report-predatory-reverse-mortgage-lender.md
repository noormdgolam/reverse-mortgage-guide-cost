The reverse mortgage industry is heavily regulated, but bad actors and aggressive salesmen still exist. 

If a loan officer is pressuring you to sign documents you don't understand, threatening you, or lying about the fees involved in the transaction, you have incredible power to stop them. Federal and state regulators take elder financial abuse extremely seriously.

Here is exactly how to report a predatory lender.

## 1. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)

The CFPB is the most feared federal watchdog in the financial industry. They have the power to levy massive, multi-million dollar fines against mortgage companies that abuse seniors.

If you submit a formal complaint on the **CFPB Website (consumerfinance.gov)**, the lender is legally required to respond to the CFPB and address your specific issue within 15 days. If a loan officer is holding your appraisal hostage or lying about your interest rate, a CFPB complaint will force the company's executive compliance team to intervene immediately.

## 2. Your State Attorney General

Every state has an Attorney General's office with a dedicated Consumer Protection division. These offices are highly aggressive when local seniors are being targeted by out-of-state telemarketing scammers. 

Filing a complaint with your State AG can trigger a statewide investigation into the lender's marketing practices. 

## 3. The HUD Office of Inspector General (OIG)

Because the FHA (a division of HUD) insures the loan, any fraud committed during a reverse mortgage transaction is a federal crime. 

If a loan officer is encouraging you to forge documents, lie on your occupancy certificate, or if a contractor is coordinating with the lender to steal your equity, you should report them directly to the **HUD OIG Hotline**. They investigate systemic fraud and can strip a lender of their license to originate FHA loans.

**The Golden Rule:** You are the customer. You are paying thousands of dollars in fees for this service. If a loan officer ever makes you feel uncomfortable, hang up the phone, walk away from the deal, and find a reputable broker.
