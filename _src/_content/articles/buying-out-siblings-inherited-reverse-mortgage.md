When parents pass away and leave a home with a reverse mortgage to multiple children, the estate settlement process can quickly become complicated. 

The most common point of friction occurs when one sibling wants to keep the family home (perhaps they were living there as a caregiver), while the other siblings want to sell the home and take their share of the cash inheritance. 

Here is exactly how a sibling buyout works when a reverse mortgage is involved.

## Step 1: Determine the Equity

First, the heirs must determine exactly how much equity is left in the property.
- Obtain an independent appraisal to determine the current fair market value.
- Request a payoff statement from the reverse mortgage servicer to see the exact loan balance.
- Subtract the loan balance from the appraised value to find the remaining equity.

**Example:**
- Appraised Value: $400,000
- Reverse Mortgage Payoff: $250,000
- Remaining Equity: $150,000
- If there are three siblings, each sibling is entitled to $50,000 in equity.

## Step 2: The Buyout Math

The sibling who wants to keep the house (Sibling A) cannot simply "take over" the reverse mortgage. The loan is immediately Due and Payable upon the death of the last borrower. 

Sibling A must pay off the $250,000 reverse mortgage to the bank. Furthermore, Sibling A must pay Sibling B and Sibling C their $50,000 shares of the equity.

Total cash Sibling A needs: **$350,000** ($250k to the bank + $100k to the siblings).

## Step 3: Securing Financing

Unless Sibling A has $350,000 in cash sitting in a savings account, they will need to apply for a traditional mortgage.

Sibling A will go to a standard mortgage lender and apply for a "refinance" or a "purchase" loan (depending on how the probate is structured in that state) for $350,000. 
- The new mortgage pays off the reverse mortgage.
- The new mortgage pays off the other siblings.
- Sibling A now owns the home outright, subject to their new traditional mortgage, and must make monthly payments.

If Sibling A cannot qualify for a $350,000 mortgage based on their own income and credit score, they cannot buy out their siblings. In this scenario, the family must sell the home on the open market, pay off the reverse mortgage, and split the remaining cash.
