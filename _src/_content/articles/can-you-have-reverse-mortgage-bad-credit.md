Because a reverse mortgage does not require the borrower to make monthly mortgage payments, the credit qualification process is vastly different from a traditional loan. 

If your credit score was destroyed by medical debt or a recent job loss, you might assume you are disqualified. The good news is that **there is no minimum FICO credit score requirement for an FHA reverse mortgage.**

You can legally obtain a reverse mortgage with a 500 credit score. However, that does not mean the lender ignores your financial history.

## The Financial Assessment

In 2015, the FHA instituted the **Financial Assessment**. Instead of looking at a raw three-digit credit score, the underwriter looks at your actual payment history over the last 24 months to determine your "willingness and capacity" to pay your bills.

The underwriter is specifically looking for:
- **Housing History:** Have you missed any mortgage payments in the last 12 months?
- **Property Taxes:** Have you been delinquent on your county property taxes?
- **Installment Debt:** Are your car payments on time?
- **Revolving Debt:** Do you routinely max out and default on credit cards?

If you have a terrible credit score because of a massive medical bill collection from three years ago, but you have paid your property taxes perfectly on time every year, the underwriter will likely approve your loan.

## The Consequence of Bad Credit: The LESA

If the underwriter determines that your credit history is a disaster—specifically, that you have a history of failing to pay property taxes or homeowners insurance—they will not necessarily deny the loan.

Instead, they will approve the loan but attach a massive penalty: **The Life Expectancy Set-Aside (LESA).**

The lender will mandate that a huge portion of your reverse mortgage proceeds (often $30,000 to $50,000) be frozen in a special escrow account. The lender will use this frozen money to pay your property taxes and insurance on your behalf for the rest of your projected lifespan. 

While a LESA guarantees you will never be foreclosed on for unpaid taxes, it drastically reduces the amount of cash you actually get to put in your pocket at closing.
