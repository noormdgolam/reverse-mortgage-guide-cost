The traditional mortgage industry is highly regulated and slow. In response, private Silicon Valley tech firms have created a modern, fast alternative for accessing home equity: **The Shared Equity Agreement**.

Companies like Point, Hometap, and Unison offer a product that competes directly with the reverse mortgage, but it operates on entirely different legal principles.

## The Mechanics of Shared Equity

A shared equity agreement is an investment contract, not a loan. 

The company gives you a lump sum of cash today (usually up to 20% of your home's current value). In exchange, they purchase an "option" on the future value of your home. 

When the contract term ends (usually 10 to 30 years), or when you sell the house, or when you die, the company takes their original investment back, **plus a massive percentage of however much the home went up in value.**

## The Pros

1. **No Age Limit:** Unlike a reverse mortgage (which requires you to be 62), a 40-year-old can use a shared equity agreement.
2. **Shared Risk:** If the housing market crashes and your home loses value, the investor shares in the loss. You owe them less than they gave you. 
3. **No Interest Rates:** Because it is not a loan, there is no compounding interest rate eating away at your equity every month.
4. **Fast Approval:** Because they are largely unregulated by federal banking laws, these companies can underwrite and fund the transaction in a matter of days, whereas an FHA reverse mortgage takes months.

## The Cons (The Hidden Math)

The math on these agreements is heavily stacked in favor of the Wall Street investor. 

If your home appreciates significantly (which real estate generally does over a 10-year period), the investor's return on investment is staggering. You are essentially giving away the most profitable asset in your portfolio. 

Furthermore, these contracts place strict liens on your property that make it nearly impossible to refinance your primary mortgage or sell the home without the investor's explicit permission. 

If you are 70 years old and intend to stay in the home for the rest of your life, the FHA-insured protections of a traditional reverse mortgage are vastly safer than entering into a venture-capital equity contract.
