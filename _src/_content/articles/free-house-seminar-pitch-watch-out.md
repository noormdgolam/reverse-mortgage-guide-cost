If you are over the age of 65, you have likely received a glossy postcard in the mail inviting you to a free steak dinner at a local country club to learn about a "secret government program for seniors."

These seminars are massive marketing funnels designed by aggressive mortgage brokers. While the product they are selling (a HECM) is legitimate, the way they sell it borders on predatory.

## The "Free House" Pitch

The seminar usually features a charismatic speaker who paints a utopian picture of retirement. They tell the audience:
- *"You will never have to pay a mortgage again!"*
- *"The government guarantees you can never be kicked out of your house!"*
- *"You get tax-free cash to go on cruises, and your kids don't have to pay a dime when you die!"*

While these statements contain kernels of truth, they are dangerously incomplete.

## What the Salesman Omits

The danger of these seminars is what the speaker *doesn't* say to the audience.
1. **They don't mention the closing costs:** They conveniently leave out that it will cost you $15,000 in fees just to access this "free" cash.
2. **They downplay the property taxes:** They will gloss over the fact that if you miss a county property tax payment, the bank will absolutely foreclose on you and evict you, destroying the "you can never be kicked out" promise.
3. **They ignore compounding interest:** They do not show the mathematical charts proving that a $100,000 loan today will become a $400,000 debt in 20 years, effectively wiping out the inheritance you hoped to leave your children.

## The High-Pressure Close

At the end of the dinner, the salesmen will swarm the tables, trying to get seniors to sign preliminary application paperwork before they leave the restaurant. 

**Rule of Thumb:** A reverse mortgage is a complex, life-altering financial decision. It should be discussed quietly in an office or at your kitchen table with your financial advisor and your children, not rushed through at a crowded steakhouse. Enjoy the free dinner, but leave your pen at home.
