---\n\nBefore you can formally apply for a Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM), federal law requires that you undergo independent counseling with a HUD-approved agency. This consumer protection measure ensures that seniors fully understand the financial implications of the loan before signing away their home equity.

## What is HUD Counseling?

The counseling session, which usually takes 60 to 90 minutes and can be done over the phone or in person, covers the pros and cons of reverse mortgages, alternative options (like downsizing or state tax deferral programs), and the financial obligations you must maintain (paying property taxes and insurance).

The counselor is an independent third party. They do not work for the lender and they do not make a commission if you close the loan. Their only job is to ensure you are making an educated decision.

## How Much Does the Counseling Cost?

Because these agencies are independent non-profits, they charge a fee for their time and services. The typical cost for a HUD counseling session ranges from **$125 to $200**.

Unlike most other closing costs (such as title insurance or origination fees), the counseling fee generally **cannot** be rolled into the reverse mortgage loan balance. It is usually an out-of-pocket expense that you must pay upfront, long before the loan is approved or closed.

## Can the Counseling Fee be Waived?

Yes. HUD requires counseling agencies to offer their services regardless of a borrower's ability to pay. If your income falls below a certain threshold (usually based on federal poverty guidelines or specific state metrics), the agency is required to waive the fee entirely.

When you call an agency to schedule your appointment, ask them immediately: *"Do you offer fee waivers for low-income seniors, and what are the income cutoffs?"*

Even if you do not qualify for a full waiver, some agencies operate on a sliding scale and may reduce the fee based on your financial hardship.

## A Warning on Lenders Paying for Counseling

You might find a lender who offers to pay the counseling fee for you. **Be extremely cautious.** Federal rules strictly prohibit lenders from paying for your counseling directly, as this creates a massive conflict of interest. The counselor must remain entirely independent. If a lender offers to pay the counselor on your behalf, they are likely violating HUD guidelines.\n
