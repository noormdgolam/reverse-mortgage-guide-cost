Michigan Seniors: Understanding the HECM Reverse Mortgage Option

A reverse mortgage is a loan product designed for homeowners 62 years of age or older, allowing them to borrow funds secured by the equity in their Michigan home.

The Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM) is the most popular type of reverse mortgage insured by the Federal Housing Administration (FHA).

How a HECM Reverse Mortgage Works:

In a HECM transaction, homeowners can choose from several payment options: a lump sum payment, a line of credit, or a combination of both.

Homeowners can also opt for the monthly payment option, receiving equal payments over a set period of time.

The loan balance grows over time, as interest accumulates and is added to the principal balance.

The total loan amount is typically 45% to 50% of your home's present value or the appraised value of your property, whichever is less.

Advantages of HECM Reverse Mortgages:

Cash flow assistance: A HECM reverse mortgage allows homeowners to receive financial assistance without selling their property or changing homeowners insurance.

No monthly mortgage payments are required, giving homeowners greater financial flexibility.

A portion of the borrowed funds may be used to improve the condition of their home.

Disadvantages of HECM Reverse Mortgages:

Interest rate fluctuations affect the loan balance growth rate.

The borrower must reside in and maintain the property as their primary residence.

As part of the HECM loan requirements, the borrower must undergo a financial assessment to ensure that they can continue making property taxes and insurance payments.

Credit Counseling and Fees:

Prior to closing, a HECM borrower must undergo counseling through a government-approved nonprofit agency to discuss loan implications and ensure the borrower understands their obligations.

Lender fees, origination fees, closing costs, and insurance premiums may be assessed and must be disclosed in the loan estimate.

Appraisal fees are the homeowner's responsibility.

Eligibility Requirements:

Candidates must be 62 years of age or older.

Owner-occupied single-family homes, 1- to 4-unit properties, and a few condominiums are eligible.

Candidates must occupy and maintain the property as their primary residence.

Applicants must demonstrate financial capacity for property taxes and insurance payments.

Making Sense of the Options:

Before applying, Michigan seniors should carefully weigh the pros and cons, consider various payment options, and explore the potential impact on their financial and tax situation.

A thorough review of available resources, such as the AARP, the National Reverse Mortgage Lenders Association (NRMLA), and the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development's (HUD) web portal, can also provide valuable information and insights.
