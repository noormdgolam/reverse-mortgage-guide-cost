Key Takeaways

  • Beware of aggressive marketing that promises a 'free house' or 'government money'.
  • A reverse mortgage is a loan, not a government grant, and it must be repaid.
  • Always consult with an independent HUD counselor, not the seminar salesman.

What is a Reverse Mortgage?

A reverse mortgage is a type of loan that allows homeowners to borrow money using the equity in their home as collateral. Unlike traditional mortgages, where borrowers make monthly payments to a lender, reverse mortgages allow borrowers to receive tax-free cash payments without making monthly mortgage payments.

How Does a Reverse Mortgage Work?

A reverse mortgage is based on the borrower's age, current interest rates, and the value of their home. The lender pays the borrower a lump sum, monthly payments, or a line of credit over the borrower's lifetime, provided the borrower continues to occupy the home as their primary residence.

Types of Reverse Mortgages

  • Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM): A HECM is the most common type of reverse mortgage offered by the Federal Housing Administration (FHA). This loan allows borrowers to use their home's equity to receive monthly payments, a lump sum, or a line of credit.
  • Purchase Loan: A purchase loan allows borrowers to buy a home using a reverse mortgage. This type of loan is usually used by retirees who want to buy a new home without making a large down payment.
  • Home Equity Loan: A home equity loan is a type of reverse mortgage that allows borrowers to borrow a fixed amount of money using the equity in their home as collateral.

Benefits of a Reverse Mortgage

  • No monthly mortgage payments are required.
  • Borrowers can use the tax-free cash payments to cover living expenses, pay off high-interest debt, or make home improvements.
  • No income tax is paid on the loan proceeds, as they are considered tax-free proceeds.
  • Non-recourse loan: Borrowers will not owe more than the value of their home.
  • Increases in equity can make it easier to qualify for government benefits, tax credits, or other programs.

Risks and Considerations

Borrowers should be aware of the following risks and considerations when considering a reverse mortgage:

  • The loan balance increases over time, reducing the amount of equity in the home.
  • Borrowers are charged origination fees, closing costs, and ongoing costs.
  • The loan becomes due when the borrower passes away, sells the property, or permanently vacates the property.
  • The loan balance, including interest and fees, is due in full, which may result in a significant debt.

Repayment Options

If the borrower cannot pay the debt after selling the property, the Federal Housing Administration will purchase the property to recoup the debt, and any equity remaining is returned to the borrower's estate.

Borrowers should work with a licensed mortgage professional and consider their personal financial situation, long-term care needs, and overall goals before applying for a reverse mortgage.

It's essential to carefully review and compare the terms and conditions of different reverse mortgage options, as interest rates and fees can vary significantly.

With the right guidance and a thorough understanding of the loan terms, reverse mortgages can be a valuable financial tool for eligible homeowners.
