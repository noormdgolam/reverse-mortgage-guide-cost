HELOC (Home Equity Line of Credit) vs. Reverse Mortgage

By Reverse Mortgage Guide Team Updated: March 01, 2024
𝕏 Share f Share in Share P Pin

Key Takeaways

  • HELOCs are cheap to set up but can be frozen or cancelled by the bank at any time.
  • HELOCs require monthly payments and fully reset after 10 years, causing a massive payment shock.
  • A HECM line of credit is guaranteed by the FHA and actually grows over time.

Understanding Home Equity Loans and Reverse Mortgages

A Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC) and a reverse mortgage are two loan options that allow homeowners to tap into their home's equity. However, these loans have significant differences, and understanding the reverse mortgage is crucial for those considering this alternative.

What is a Reverse Mortgage?

A reverse mortgage is a type of loan that allows homeowners to borrow money using the equity in their home as collateral. Unlike traditional loans, reverse mortgages do not require monthly payments, and the loan balance grows over time as interest accrues.

The three main types of reverse mortgages are:

  • Home Equity Conversion Mortgage (HECM), insured by the Federal Housing Administration (FHA), is the most common type of reverse mortgage.
  • Proprietary Reverse Mortgages come from private companies, such as Financial Freedom and Bank of America.
  • Pennsylvania Reverse Mortgages are also available for residents in Pennsylvania and have unique requirements.

Reverse mortgages offer several benefits, including:

  • No monthly mortgage payments, which can help reduce expenses for retirees or those on a fixed income.
  • No income or credit score requirements, making it accessible to a wide range of homeowners.
  • No taxes on the loan proceeds; however, the interest and fees associated with the loan are tax-deductible.

Key Considerations and Risks

While a reverse mortgage can be a valuable financial tool, there are key considerations and risks to be aware of:

  • Accumulating debt and the risk of owing more than the home's value.
  • Loss of home ownership or the risk of foreclosure if the loan is not repaid.
  • Tax implications, as the interest and fees associated with the loan may increase taxes.

Eligibility and Application Process

To be eligible for a reverse mortgage, homeowners must:

  • Be 62 years or older and a permanent resident of the United States.
  • Own their home outright or have a low balance on their mortgage with sufficient equity.
  • Live in the home as their primary residence and occupy it as their main home.

The application process typically involves:

  • Meeting with a counselor to discuss the loan options and implications.
  • Filling out the loan application and providing required documentation.
  • The lender conducting an appraisal of the home to determine its value.

Conclusion

A reverse mortgage is a unique loan option that can provide financial flexibility for homeowners. However, it is essential to carefully evaluate the benefits and risks before making a decision. Homeowners should consult with a financial advisor, a reverse mortgage expert, or a social worker before applying for a reverse mortgage.
